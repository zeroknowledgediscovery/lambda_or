\section*{Response to Reviewer 1}

\paragraph{Comment.}
The motivation of the study is unclear because of the poor presentation. Description of the introduction is not adequate for readers to understand the problem setup and what situation the authors consider. The brief summary in section 1.1 does not help understanding because of the lack of detailed explanations for readers without prior knowledge.

\paragraph{Response.}
We thank the reviewer for this assessment. In response, we substantially rewrote the Introduction and the opening of Section 1.1 to make the problem setup more explicit and easier to follow for readers encountering the framework for the first time. The revised text now explains earlier and more clearly what setting we consider, why proxy-labeling arises naturally in this setting, and what practical problem the proposed estimator is intended to solve. We also reduced the density of the opening exposition and moved the intuitive interpretation ahead of the more formal discussion so that the reader can understand the objective of the method before encountering the technical details.

\paragraph{Comment.}
``Consider retrospective dataset with binary case/control labels where we have on hand a predictive model \(f\), and a corresponding ROC curve.'' I cannot understand what the setup is. What do the binary case/control labels mean? According to the following description, \(Y\) seems to be not the binary case/control labels. Then, it is unclear how the binary case/control labels is used.

\paragraph{Response.}
We agree that the original wording was ambiguous. We revised the setup in Section 2.1 so that the roles of the true outcome \(Y\), the predictive score \(S=f(\mathbf{Z})\), the proxy labels induced from the score tails, and the selected sample are now separated explicitly. In the revised manuscript, the reader is told directly which quantity is latent, which quantity is observed, and how the pre-existing predictive model is used to construct high-purity proxy strata. This clarification was added precisely to remove the confusion between the true case/control status and the score-derived proxy labeling mechanism.

\paragraph{Comment.}
Definition of eq. (2) is imcomplete. In particular, in the sentence after equation (2), ``where \(\tilde{a}\) is the count with \(X = 1\) and labeled positive, and so on'', the important definitions of \(\tilde{b}\), \(\tilde{c}\) and \(\tilde{d}\) are omitted, which makes understanding of the statistical model impossible. Moreover, what is the probability that the \(\tilde{T}\) is observed? Is this from the retrospective sampling? The population versions of \(T\) and \(\tilde{T}\) are needed.

\paragraph{Response.}
We thank the reviewer for identifying this omission. In the revised manuscript, all entries of both the observed proxy contingency table and the latent true contingency table are now defined explicitly. We also labeled rows and columns directly and added text clarifying the expected-count relationship between the latent table and the observed proxy table through the misclassification operator. This revision makes clear that the key relationship is formulated at the population or expectation level within the selected set, which is the level at which the correction is derived. These additions were made specifically to ensure that the table construction is fully interpretable without relying on implicit notation.

\paragraph{Comment.}
In eq. (4), \(T\) is unclearly defined. What does ``the unknow true count matrix'' mean?
From eq. (4), for example, the \((1,1)\)-element of \(\tilde{T}\), \(\tilde{T}_{11}\), can be written as:
\[
\tilde{T}_{11} = \{T K^T\}_{11} = T_{11} p_{sel} + T_{12} (1-p_{sel}) = T_{11} P(\tilde{Y}=1 \mid Y=1,I=1) + T_{12} P(\tilde{Y}=0 \mid Y=1,I=1)
\]
Given the above expression, I cannot understand what the \(\tilde{T}_{11}\) means and what \(T_{11}\) and \(T_{12}\) are appropriate.

\paragraph{Response.}
We agree that the earlier wording was too compressed. In the revised manuscript, \(T\) is now defined directly as the latent contingency table indexed by exposure and the true outcome within the selected set, while \(\tilde{T}\) is defined as the corresponding observed contingency table formed using the proxy labels. We also rewrote the surrounding explanation so that the indexing of the entries and the action of the operator \(K\) are described in words before the matrix equation is used. The goal of this revision was to make the interpretation of entries such as \(\tilde{T}_{11}\), \(T_{11}\), and \(T_{12}\) transparent even for readers who do not work routinely with matrix representations of misclassification.

\paragraph{Comment.}
There is no justfication of the ridge regularization. I can understand that the authors try to avoid negative or inflated values of the estimated contingency table, but justification in terms of statistical models or maximum likelihood estimation is lacking.
Please note that there exists Firth-penalized method (Firth 1993) that can be applied to any maximum likelihood estimation methods.
It is well known that the Firth-penalized logistic regression avoids infinite odds ratio estimation just like the authors' motivation. Why not consider the Firth-penalized method for the current setup?

\paragraph{Response.}
We appreciate this important point and revised the manuscript to make the distinction much more explicit. In the revised Methods, we now explain that the ridge term is introduced to stabilize an upstream inverse problem, namely reconstruction of the latent contingency table when the misclassification operator is close to singular. This is different from Firth penalization, which addresses downstream separation and small-sample bias in likelihood-based regression. In other words, the two regularizations act at different stages of the pipeline and solve different problems. We expanded the text in Section 2.2 to explain why the ridge-stabilized inverse is the relevant device in our setting and why a Firth correction would not replace the need to regularize the inversion step itself.

\paragraph{Comment.}
It is unclear why the SHAP is considered. Connection between SHAP and odds ratio are unclear.

\paragraph{Response.}
We revised the SHAP comparison section to state much more clearly why it is included. In the revised manuscript, we no longer rely on an implicit connection between SHAP values and odds ratios. Instead, we explain directly that SHAP is presented as a widely used attribution baseline in applied machine learning, against which the proposed method can be contrasted in terms of stability and interpretability under outcome misclassification. The revised text now makes clear that the comparison is contextual and empirical, not based on any claim that SHAP and odds-ratio estimators are mathematically equivalent.

\paragraph{Comment.}
It is unclear how \(T\) and \(\tilde{T}\) are defined.
In the python script ``lambdaORsimulation.py'' from the provided github link, it seems that \(Y\) is generated given \(X\) though a logistic regression model unlike the retrospective sampling mentioned in the paper to generate \(X\) given \(Y\). Please correct me if I misunderstand.

\paragraph{Response.}
We thank the reviewer for pointing out this source of confusion. In the revised manuscript, we clarified the definitions of \(T\) and \(\tilde{T}\) throughout the Methods and made the generative purpose of the simulation section more explicit. The simulation is used to study estimator behavior under controlled data-generating conditions that reproduce the misclassification structure relevant to the theory; it is not intended to literally mimic every operational detail of retrospective sampling. We added text to the simulation section to explain this distinction more carefully so that the role of the simulation design is clear.

\paragraph{Comment.}
A central limitation is that the proposed approach seems to hinge on the availability of a strong, well-calibrated predictive model to define the tail strata and to estimate . Could the authors clarify how sensitive the method’s performance is to the quality of this predictive model (e.g., varying AUC/calibration and validation-set shift)? In particular, under what conditions does \(\lambda\)-OR provide meaningful improvement over the naive OR, and when does it degrade or become unstable?

\paragraph{Response.}
We thank the reviewer for this important point. In the revision, we addressed this issue through the theory rather than by adding an intentionally poor-model simulation. The revised manuscript now makes clearer that estimator behavior depends on the conditioning of the misclassification operator \(K\), which is determined by the tail-specific operating characteristics. As upstream discrimination deteriorates and approaches chance performance, the operator becomes ill-conditioned, the variance increases, and the corrected estimator correspondingly loses precision or collapses conservatively rather than becoming spuriously confident. We chose to address this issue analytically because that framework already characterizes the relevant degradation mechanism directly.
